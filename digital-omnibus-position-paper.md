This document summarizes the key concerns and proposals raised by Axeptio regarding the consent-related provisions of the Digital Omnibus, published by the European Commission on 19 November 2025. The Omnibus proposes to move cookie and terminal-equipment consent rules from the ePrivacy Directive into the GDPR (new Articles 88a and 88b), introduce machine-readable consent signals, and require browser providers to build consent-management capabilities directly into their products. While we share the Commission's objective of reducing consent fatigue, we believe several provisions, as currently drafted, risk achieving the opposite of their stated aim — and would concentrate critical power in the hands of a small number of non-European gatekeepers.
It is worth noting that these concerns are widely shared across the European CMP industry. Axeptio has been coordinating with other European CMP providers — including iubenda, Usercentrics, Pandectes, MyAgilePrivacy, SecurePrivacy, CommandersAct, SirData, and CookieHub — within the framework of a nascent European CMP Association. While this document reflects Axeptio's position alone, the convergence of views across the industry on these key issues is striking and, we believe, significant.

I. Consent Centralization in the Browser: A Transfer of Power to Big Tech
Article 88b(6) requires non-SME browser providers to provide the technical means for users to give or refuse consent through automated, machine-readable signals. In practice, this means four companies — Google (Chrome), Apple (Safari), Microsoft (Edge), and Mozilla (funded by Google, Firefox) — would design, build, and control the consent experience for virtually all European internet users.
This is not a neutral technical choice. The design of a consent interface determines outcomes. We have already seen this play out twice in recent years. Google's Privacy Sandbox initiative and Apple's App Tracking Transparency both led to antitrust proceedings precisely because their consent designs gave their own services preferential treatment. In both cases, the party controlling the consent UI retained full access to first-party data while cutting off competitors who depend on user consent.
A browser-level blanket consent toggle — "accept tracking" or "refuse tracking" — would benefit platforms that already have logged-in users (Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon) and devastate the open web: independent publishers, e-commerce sites, European SaaS companies, and analytics providers who rely on informed, specific consent to operate.
Centralizing consent in the browser is acceptable only if the design is not left to browser vendors alone. The text as drafted would hand the keys to companies whose business models create an inherent conflict of interest.

II. The Real Causes of Consent Fatigue
The Commission's impact assessment estimates that EU citizens spend 334 million hours on cookie banners per year. This is a real problem — but the diagnosis must be accurate for the remedy to work.
Consent fatigue is not primarily caused by the existence of consent banners. It is caused by three technical and structural factors that the Digital Omnibus does not address:
Browser tracking protection clears consent state. Technologies like Safari's Intelligent Tracking Protection and Firefox's Enhanced Tracking Protection regularly delete the cookies that CMPs use to remember a user's consent choice. The result: users who already made a choice are asked again, sometimes within days. This is the single largest driver of repeated consent prompts, and it is entirely within the control of the same browser vendors the Omnibus would empower.
In-app browsers (webviews) have no storage access. A growing share of mobile browsing happens inside apps — clicking a link in Instagram, LinkedIn, or a news app opens an embedded browser that is sandboxed from the device's main browser. These webviews cannot access previously stored consent. Every page visit appears as a fresh user. This problem is growing, is entirely caused by platform design choices, and is absent from the Digital Omnibus.
Poor CMP implementations and manipulative design patterns. Not all consent banners are created equal. Some are deliberately confusing, use dark patterns, or present hundreds of "partners" behind opaque toggles. The solution is better CMP quality and enforcement — not eliminating CMPs in favor of a browser toggle that removes all contextual information.
When we presented these technical realities to the Commission alongside ten other CMP providers, several of these points were new to their teams. We believe the Parliament should examine them closely before accepting the Commission's framing.

III. The Media Exemption Undermines the Entire Framework
Recital 46 and related provisions exempt media service providers from the obligation to respect machine-readable consent signals. The stated rationale is to protect independent journalism.
However, media websites are precisely where consent fatigue is most acute. News sites typically load dozens of advertising vendors, present the most complex consent interfaces, and deploy cookie walls followed by paywalls — the combination users find most frustrating. Exempting this entire sector from the consent signal framework means the most problematic consent experiences would continue unchanged, while simpler, less intrusive sites (e-commerce, SaaS, corporate sites) bear the full burden of the new regime.
We support targeted measures to sustain independent journalism. But a blanket media exemption from consent signals creates a perverse outcome: the Digital Omnibus would reduce friction where it is already low and preserve it where it is highest.

IV. Signal Standardization: Interoperability, Not Uniformity
Article 88b(4) tasks European standardization organizations with drafting standards for machine-readable consent signals. We strongly support this direction — but with important caveats.
A consent signal must be specific and informed to satisfy Article 4(11) GDPR. A blanket "yes to tracking" or "no to tracking" signal cannot meet this standard, because consent must relate to specific purposes and specific data processors. A user who trusts their bank's analytics provider has no reason to apply the same preference to an unknown retargeting vendor on a news site. The specificity requirement is not a technicality — it is the core of what makes consent meaningful under European law.
We therefore advocate for a standard that enables interoperability between consent assistants and CMPs, rather than a rigid, one-size-fits-all signal. The standard should allow data processors to express their processing activities in structured, machine-readable form, and allow consent assistants to react to that information with granular, context-aware decisions.
CMP providers must participate in the standardization process. We combine deep legal expertise with hands-on technical knowledge of consent collection and signaling, and are uniquely positioned to help shape workable, compliant specifications. We also urge caution regarding the IAB Transparency and Consent Framework (TCF) as a template — it is governed by a sectoral association, represents only a fraction of use cases (publisher advertising), and is unsuitable for e-commerce, SaaS, landing pages, and most other online services.

V. Our Proposal: A Competitive Market for Privacy-Enhancing Consent Assistants
Rather than entrusting consent to browser vendors, we propose a three-layer architecture that preserves user choice, enables innovation, and maintains legal compliance:
Layer 1 — A standardized browser API (navigator.consent). Browser providers would implement a thin, neutral API — similar to existing APIs like navigator.geolocation — that allows CMPs and consent assistants to communicate through the browser. This API would not itself make consent decisions. It would provide the plumbing. We have drafted a detailed RFC for this API, which we would be happy to share with the Parliament's technical services.
Layer 2 — Third-party consent assistant extensions, selected by the user. Upon installing or updating a browser, users would be presented with a choice of privacy assistant extensions — much like the search engine selection screen mandated under the Digital Markets Act. These extensions would use the browser API to communicate with CMPs, retrieve vendor and purpose information, and apply the user's preferences granularly and contextually. Several such extensions already exist: Consent-o-matic (developed by Aarhus University), SuperAgent, Consenter, and Taste by Axeptio. This model creates a competitive European market for privacy innovation, rather than a monopoly for four American companies.
Layer 3 — CMPs continue to operate as the compliance layer. CMPs would remain responsible for what they do best: providing contextual information about data processing, collecting and recording consent with audit trails, translating user preferences into vendor-level technical instructions, and ensuring regulatory compliance. A browser signal alone cannot fulfill these functions. CMPs are not the banner — they are the underlying system that informs users, records choices, applies them across vendors, and proves compliance.
This architecture is not theoretical. We have built the technical foundation and are actively opening it to the industry:
CMP Agents is an open-source JavaScript library developed by Axeptio that provides a unified interface to interact with 40+ different CMPs (including CookieBot, Didomi, OneTrust, UserCentrics, TrustArc, iubenda, and many more). It enables any consent assistant to detect, read, and communicate with any CMP — a "Rosetta Stone" for the consent ecosystem. The library is being opened to members of the European CMP Association under a license that grants free use to CMP industry peers, consent assistant developers, and researchers.
The navigator.consent RFC is a detailed technical specification for a browser-level API that would formalize the communication channel between CMPs and consent assistants. It defines methods for CMP registration, vendor and purpose declaration, preference exchange, and event-driven updates — all with security safeguards ensuring that only browser extensions (not arbitrary page scripts) can access the consent assistant API surface.
Together, these building blocks demonstrate that the interoperable, competitive, user-empowering model we advocate is not a distant aspiration — it is ready for deployment and standardization.

VI. Summary of Axeptio's Position on Key Provisions
Consent centralization (Art. 88b(6)): We support enabling browser-level consent management, but only if it is open to independent third-party consent assistant extensions — not controlled exclusively by browser vendors. The text should mandate a choice screen for privacy assistants and ensure CMPs remain complementary.
Signal standardization (Art. 88b(4)): We strongly support an open EU standard for machine-readable consent signals. CMPs must participate in drafting the standard. The standard must preserve the specificity and informed nature of consent required by GDPR Article 4(11).
Media exemption (Recital 46): Any exemption must be narrowly defined. A broad media exemption undermines the entire consent signal framework and leaves the most frustrating consent experiences untouched.
Subsequent consent requests (Art. 88a(b)-(c)): Reducing repeated prompts is positive, but technically unfeasible without addressing browser tracking protection and webview sandboxing. The six-month rule must allow re-consent when processing activities materially change (new vendors, new purposes).
ePrivacy-to-GDPR migration: We express concern about moving terminal-equipment rules into the GDPR. Article 5(3) of the ePrivacy Directive is a clear, technology-specific safeguard. Replacing it with a risk-based approach within the GDPR risks ambiguity and opens the door to creative reinterpretation that weakens protection.

VII. What We Ask of the Parliament
We respectfully ask the Parliament to consider the following during its examination of the Digital Omnibus:
Amend Article 88b(6) to require that browser-level consent mechanisms be open to independent, third-party consent assistant extensions — not limited to browser vendors' built-in functionality. Consider mandating a choice screen for privacy assistants, as was done for search engines under the DMA.
Ensure CMP industry participation in the standardization process under Article 88b(4), and specify that the standard must support granular, purpose-specific, and vendor-specific consent — not blanket signals.
Narrow the media exemption to prevent it from becoming a loophole that exempts the most consent-heavy sector from the framework's core mechanism.
Investigate and address the root technical causes of consent fatigue — browser tracking protection, webview sandboxing, and poor CMP implementations — which the current text ignores entirely.
Preserve the clarity of the ePrivacy framework's terminal-equipment protections rather than dissolving them into the GDPR's broader, more ambiguous risk-based approach.

We remain fully available to the Parliament and your office for technical briefings, demonstrations of the technology described above, or any further exchange. Axeptio is committed to being part of the solution to consent fatigue — and we believe the solution lies in innovation, competition, and interoperability, not in centralizing control with the very platforms this legislation should be holding accountable.
